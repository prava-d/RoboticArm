The Power of the 3D Printing

Benjamin Ziemann :: October 20, 2017

When we started this project we knew that we would be prototyping a lot, especially in terms of finger design to meet our goal of a human like motion. Because of this we needed a process to quickly design, fabricate, and modify our critical parts. After a quick brainstorm we settled on the use of 3D printing.

Allowing us to make almost any shape of reasonable size for our project in just a few hours, this solved a lot of problems in terms of fabrication. Besides the standard polylactic acid (PLA) plastic, we also had access to thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU), a flexible filament which varies itâ€™s flexibility with infill density and shape it is printed in. This gave us the perfect leaping off point for testing a single part finger.

However we quickly realized that we could combine the two plastics into a single print, allowing us to create a flexible backbone while having rigid fingers. Because we did not have access to a multi-extruder printer, we did this by simply switching out the filament at the right time. However this meant that the material had to be the same for each layer.

Our first pass of this failed as the infill at the switching of the two layers was too little, causing the layer between the two filaments to break. Using Simplify3D (thanks Hannah for letting us use your account!), we could set the intermediate layers between the two filaments to be greater, allowing for more surface area and a better hold to form between the layers.

Ultimately we moved away from this model due to wanting increased dexterity and control of the finger but continued to use 3D printing for all our finger prototypes to data.

While it may not be in the final project, 3D printed parts provided us with the speed, flexibility (pun intended), and precision we needed to flesh out our ideas in a real show of rapid prototyping.
